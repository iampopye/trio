# Brand Voice Analysis and Guidelines

Analyze a brand's voice, tone, and messaging across channels and generate a comprehensive brand voice guidelines document that any writer can follow.

## When to Use

- Documenting or understanding a brand's voice
- Creating brand voice guidelines for a team or agency
- Ensuring consistency across marketing channels
- Rebranding or refining brand identity
- Comparing brand voice to competitors

## Step 1: Gather Source Material

Analyze content from multiple sources in priority order:

**Primary Sources (must analyze):**
1. Homepage -- the most curated brand representation
2. About page -- how the brand describes itself
3. Product/service pages -- how they present offerings

**Secondary Sources:**
4. Blog posts (3-5 recent)
5. Social media profiles and recent posts
6. Email newsletters
7. Customer-facing copy (error messages, onboarding, help docs)

**Tertiary Sources:**
8. Job postings (reveals internal culture)
9. Press releases
10. Ad copy
11. Video scripts or podcast transcripts

## Step 2: Voice Dimension Analysis

Map the brand's voice along four dimensions (each scored 1-10):

### Formal <-----> Casual
| Signal | Formal | Casual |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Contractions | Avoids ("do not", "cannot") | Uses freely ("don't", "can't") |
| Sentence structure | Complex, longer | Short, punchy |
| Vocabulary | Professional, industry-standard | Conversational, everyday |
| Pronouns | Third person ("the company") | First/second person ("we", "you") |
| Humor | Rare or absent | Frequent, natural |

### Serious <-----> Playful
| Signal | Serious | Playful |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Tone | Authoritative, measured | Light-hearted, fun |
| Metaphors | Rare, conservative | Creative, unexpected |
| Emoji use | Never | Sometimes or often |
| Error messages | "An error has occurred" | "Oops! Something went sideways" |

### Technical <-----> Simple
| Signal | Technical | Simple |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Jargon | Uses freely | Avoids or explains |
| Detail level | In-depth | High-level overviews |
| Examples | Complex, domain-specific | Simple, relatable analogies |

### Reserved <-----> Bold
| Signal | Reserved | Bold |
|--------|----------|------|
| Claims | Hedged ("we believe") | Direct ("we guarantee") |
| Opinions | Neutral, balanced | Strong, opinionated |
| Competitive refs | Avoids mentioning competitors | Directly compares |

**Evidence required:** Quote 3-5 specific examples from source material to support each rating.

## Step 3: Brand Personality Archetypes

Map the brand to one of five core archetypes (brands may blend 1-2):

**1. The Authority** -- Expert, trustworthy, data-driven (Finance, healthcare, B2B enterprise)
**2. The Innovator** -- Forward-thinking, disruptive, visionary (Tech, SaaS, startups)
**3. The Friend** -- Warm, approachable, helpful, relatable (Consumer products, education)
**4. The Rebel** -- Bold, challenging conventions, irreverent (Lifestyle, fitness, DTC)
**5. The Guide** -- Wise, patient, methodical, trustworthy (Education, professional tools)

## Step 4: Vocabulary Analysis

### Words They Use Frequently
- **Action words** (verbs they favor)
- **Descriptive words** (adjectives they use)
- **Value words** (reflecting their values)
- **Industry-specific terms**

### Words They Avoid
- Words too casual or too technical for the brand
- Competitor terminology they deliberately avoid
- Industry cliches they sidestep

### Signature Phrases
- Tagline, recurring phrases, linguistic patterns

## Step 5: Competitor Voice Comparison

| Dimension | Brand | Competitor 1 | Competitor 2 | Competitor 3 |
|-----------|-------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Formal <> Casual | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 |
| Serious <> Playful | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 |
| Technical <> Simple | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 |
| Reserved <> Bold | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 | X/10 |

Identify where voices overlap (differentiation opportunity) and what voice territory is unoccupied.

## Step 6: Consistency Audit

Assess voice consistency across all analyzed channels (Consistent / Mostly / Inconsistent). Common issues include:
- Different writers creating different tones
- Social media voice drastically different from website
- Old pages not updated to match current voice

## Step 7: Brand Messaging Hierarchy

1. **Tagline** (under 10 words) -- most compressed brand message
2. **Value Propositions** (1 sentence each) -- 3-5 core value props
3. **Elevator Pitch** (30 seconds / 75 words)
4. **Boilerplate** (100-150 words) -- standard about paragraph
5. **Full Brand Story** (300-500 words) -- complete narrative

## Step 8: Voice Chart and Guidelines

```
OUR VOICE IS:                    OUR VOICE IS NOT:
--------------------------------------------------
[Characteristic 1]              [Anti-characteristic 1]
e.g., "Confident"               e.g., "Arrogant"
[Characteristic 2]              [Anti-characteristic 2]
e.g., "Helpful"                 e.g., "Condescending"
```

### Writing Do's and Don'ts
- Specific, actionable instructions based on the analysis
- Concrete examples for each guideline

## Step 9: Copy Samples

Provide 5-8 sample pieces written in the identified brand voice:
1. Homepage Headline
2. Product Description Paragraph
3. Blog Post Opening
4. Social Media Post
5. Email Subject Line
6. CTA Button Text
7. Error Message
8. Customer Thank You Message

## Output Format

Generate a comprehensive brand voice document covering:
- Voice Summary
- Voice Dimensions with visual spectrum map
- Brand Personality archetype assessment
- Tone by Context table
- Vocabulary lists
- Voice Chart (IS / IS NOT)
- Writing Guidelines (Do's and Don'ts)
- Brand Messaging Hierarchy
- Copy Samples
- Competitor Voice Comparison
- Consistency Audit with score
- Recommendations for improvement

## Key Principles

- Always provide EVIDENCE for every assessment with specific quoted examples
- The guide should be usable by someone who has never worked with the brand
- Copy samples are the most valuable part -- people learn voice by example
- Voice is the consistent personality; tone shifts by context
- Frame consistency issues as opportunities, not failures
